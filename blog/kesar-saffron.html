Kesar saffron. What it is, where it comes from, and how to use it

Kesar is the South Asian name for saffron, used across India, Pakistan, and the diaspora for thousands of years. A complete guide to what kesar actually is, how Afghan and Kashmiri kesar compare, and how it is used in biryani, kheer, and kesar doodh.

Kesar is the Hindi, Urdu, and Punjabi word for saffron. It comes from the Sanskrit word kunkuma, which referred to saffron as a dye, a ritual ingredient, and a culinary spice across the Indian subcontinent for thousands of years. When South Asian households refer to kesar, they are referring to the same Crocus sativus stigmas that Persian cooks call zafaran and Afghan families use in their kahwa. The ingredient is identical. The name reflects centuries of distinct cultural relationship with it.

Understanding what kesar actually is, its quality markers, its traditional uses, and how to identify genuine product, is increasingly relevant as the global saffron market fills with adulterated and mislabelled products. Kesar sold in South Asian grocery stores varies enormously in quality, and the cultural significance of the ingredient in South Asian cooking makes getting the right product genuinely important.

What Kesar Actually Is

Kesar is the dried stigma of the Crocus sativus flower. Each flower produces three stigmas, which must be hand-picked during a narrow harvesting window in autumn. It takes approximately 75,000 to 150,000 flowers to produce one kilogram of dried saffron. This labour intensity is the primary reason kesar commands the price it does. It is not a manufactured scarcity, it is a genuine reflection of the work involved in producing it.

The quality of kesar is determined by three measurable compounds. Crocin produces the characteristic deep golden colour that kesar gives to rice, desserts, and milk. Picrocrocin produces the slightly bitter, complex flavour. Safranal produces the distinctive floral aroma that fills a kitchen when real kesar is bloomed. These three compounds together are what make kesar irreplaceable in the dishes that depend on it.

The international standard for saffron quality is ISO 3632, which divides saffron into four grades based on measured crocin, picrocrocin, and safranal levels. Grade I is the highest category, requiring a minimum crocin reading of 200. Premium Afghan kesar from the Herat province, classified as Super Negin grade, consistently achieves crocin readings above 260 and often significantly higher. This is meaningfully more potent than the minimum Grade I threshold.

Kashmir Kesar vs Afghan Kesar

The most historically significant kesar producing region within the Indian subcontinent is the Pampore area of Kashmir, where saffron cultivation dates back several centuries. Kashmiri kesar has a strong cultural identity and commands premium prices in the Indian market. It is grown at high altitude in a specific microclimate that produces saffron with a distinctive character.

However, Kashmiri saffron production has declined significantly in recent decades due to changing water table levels, reduced snowfall, and agricultural pressure on the growing land. The supply of genuine Kashmiri kesar is limited and expensive, and the market is heavily adulterated with Iranian and Afghan saffron sold under the Kashmiri label. A 2015 study by the Indian Spices Board found that a significant proportion of saffron sold as Kashmiri origin was not actually grown in Kashmir.

Afghan kesar from the Herat province has emerged as the quality benchmark in recent years, with laboratory measurements consistently showing among the highest crocin levels of any saffron growing region in the world. Afghanistan overtook Iran as the world's second largest saffron producer within the past decade, and the quality of Herat province saffron is now recognised internationally. For South Asian buyers seeking genuine premium kesar, Afghan origin is worth understanding as a serious alternative to the Kashmiri label.

How Kesar Is Used in South Asian Cooking

Kesar appears across South Asian cuisine in ways that reflect its dual role as both a flavouring and a marker of celebration and care. The most common applications are in rice dishes, desserts, and dairy preparations.

Biryani is the most widely recognised kesar application. The saffron is typically bloomed in warm milk and drizzled over the top layer of rice during the dum cooking process, creating the characteristic golden patches and fragrant steam that define a proper biryani. The kesar in biryani is not incidental. It is the ingredient that distinguishes a celebratory dish from an everyday one.

Kheer, the South Asian rice pudding, uses kesar as its primary flavouring alongside cardamom. Sheer khurma, the vermicelli milk pudding prepared for Eid in Pakistani and Afghan households, centres on kesar as its defining ingredient. Rasmalai and gulab jamun are frequently finished with kesar-infused milk. In each case the kesar is not background. It is the primary flavour note that the dish is built around.

Kesar doodh, warm saffron milk, is among the most culturally significant preparations. Traditionally given to brides before weddings, to new mothers postpartum, to children before bed, and to the unwell as a warming and nourishing drink, kesar doodh represents saffron's role in South Asian domestic life as something between a culinary ingredient and a domestic medicine. The fat content of whole milk improves the bioavailability of crocin, which is why the traditional milk-based preparation likely produces a more noticeable effect than water-based preparations.

The quality of kesar determines everything in dishes where it is the primary flavour. A kheer or biryani made with genuine Grade I kesar at the correct quantity produces a dish that is immediately recognisable as properly made. The same dish with low-grade or adulterated product is simply a pale version of itself.

How to Identify Real Kesar

The most reliable test for genuine kesar is the water test. Place five to seven threads in a small bowl of warm water and leave for ten to fifteen minutes without stirring. Real kesar gradually releases a deep golden colour from the tip of the thread downward. The thread itself retains its red colour. The water should be a clear, rich gold with a distinct floral aroma.

Adulterated kesar, including threads dyed with artificial colourants or made from safflower, releases colour immediately and completely, turning the water an orange-red rather than gold. The thread itself loses its colour rapidly and becomes pale or white. There is no aroma, or only a faint chemical smell.

The price test is also informative. Genuine Grade I kesar costs between $10 and $30 per gram at retail. Product sold at $2 to $5 per gram cannot be genuine high-grade saffron given the production economics. Extremely low prices are the most reliable single indicator of adulteration.

How Much Kesar to Use

For biryani serving six to eight people: twenty to twenty-five threads bloomed in two tablespoons of warm milk for fifteen minutes, then drizzled over the top rice layer. For kheer or other milk desserts serving four: fifteen to twenty threads. For kesar doodh per cup: eight to twelve threads bloomed directly in the warm milk. For kesar rice serving four: twelve to fifteen threads bloomed in warm water.

Grade I kesar at these quantities produces a visibly golden result with clear aroma. If you find yourself needing significantly more threads to achieve colour and flavour, the quality of the kesar is almost certainly the issue rather than the quantity. Real Super Negin grade kesar at the thread counts above is sufficient for the full effect in any of these applications.
