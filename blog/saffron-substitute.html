What can you substitute for saffron. An honest answer

No ingredient fully replicates what saffron does. Some come closer than others. Here is what each substitute actually delivers, and when the honest answer is simply to buy the real thing.

April 13, 2026 · 6 minute read · By the Azaad Saffron Team · Updated April 13, 2026
Home › Journal › Saffron Substitute
← Back to The Journal

If you cook regularly with saffron, you have probably been in this position. The recipe calls for saffron and you do not have any, or what you have has been sitting in a cupboard for too long and smells of almost nothing. The question of what to substitute for saffron is one of the most searched queries in the spice category, and the honest answer is more nuanced than most results give it credit for.

The Short Answer: Nothing Fully Substitutes for Saffron

Before listing alternatives, the most useful thing to say is this: no ingredient replicates what saffron actually does. Saffron contributes three distinct things to a dish simultaneously. A specific golden colour from its crocin content. A distinctly bitter and earthy flavour from picrocrocin. And a complex floral aroma from safranal. No single spice delivers all three. Every substitute addresses one or two of these at best.

This is worth stating clearly because many substitute guides imply you will not notice the difference. If you are cooking tahdig for a Persian family, or biryani for someone who grew up eating it, they will notice. The substitutes below are practical solutions for casual cooking or recipes where saffron is a minor background note. They are not solutions for dishes where saffron is the primary flavour identity.

Turmeric: The Most Common Substitute

Turmeric is the most frequently recommended saffron substitute and the most misleading one. It replicates the colour reasonably well, producing a golden yellow in liquid and rice, but the similarity ends there. Turmeric has a warm, slightly bitter, earthy flavour that is completely different from saffron's floral character. It does not dissolve into dishes the way saffron does. Used in excess it dominates and tastes medicinal.

Where turmeric works: plain rice dishes where colour is the primary goal, soups and stews where the flavour will be masked by other spices, and any recipe where saffron is purely aesthetic. Use approximately one quarter teaspoon of ground turmeric to replace a generous pinch of saffron for colour purposes. Do not use it in saffron tea, bastani, sholeh zard, or any preparation where saffron's aroma is the point.

Safflower: The Most Confusing Substitute

Safflower is worth covering here because it is also the most common saffron adulterant. Many products sold as saffron actually contain safflower threads dyed to resemble saffron stigmas. As a deliberate substitute, safflower threads produce a yellow colour in liquid and have almost no distinct flavour. They are sometimes sold as Mexican saffron or poor man's saffron in Latin American cooking traditions.

Safflower can stand in for saffron in rice dishes where colour is the goal and no flavour contribution is needed. It will not produce the complex aroma that defines Persian rice. It is a colour additive, not a flavour one. The confusion between safflower and saffron is one reason the saffron market has such a significant adulteration problem.

Annatto: A Better Colour Option

Annatto seeds, also sold as achiote, produce a deeper orange-yellow colour than turmeric and have a subtle, slightly nutty flavour that is less intrusive in savoury dishes. It is widely used in Latin American and Filipino cuisine. For rice dishes where you need colour without the medicinal edge of turmeric, annatto is a cleaner option.

Steep a small amount of annatto seeds in warm oil or water to extract the colour, then add the strained liquid to your dish. The flavour contribution is mild enough that it does not significantly alter the dish profile. Like turmeric and safflower, it contributes nothing to the aroma dimension of saffron.

Marigold Petals: The Traditional European Substitute

Dried marigold petals, specifically Calendula officinalis, were historically used across Europe as a saffron substitute when the real spice was unavailable. They produce a pale yellow colour and have a faintly bitter, slightly floral note that comes closer to saffron's character than turmeric does, though still noticeably different.

Imereti saffron, a common ingredient in Georgian cuisine, is actually dried marigold petals rather than Crocus sativus. It is a legitimate and historically grounded ingredient in its own right. It is not a flavour equivalent to genuine saffron but it is arguably the most graceful substitute for dishes where the cultural context does not require the real thing.

A Combination Approach

Some cooks combine a small amount of turmeric for colour with a pinch of sweet paprika for warmth and a few dried marigold petals for floral character. This combination comes closer to mimicking the overall sensory impression of saffron than any single ingredient, though it still does not replicate the specific bitterness that experienced cooks will notice immediately.

When to Buy Real Saffron Instead

The search for a saffron substitute is often driven by the assumption that real saffron is unavailable or too expensive. Neither is necessarily true. A 0.5g tin of Grade I saffron provides enough for multiple dishes and costs less than a restaurant meal. It also lasts significantly longer than most people expect when stored correctly in a cool, dark place.

The dishes that depend on saffron deserve the real ingredient. If you are making tahdig, sholeh zard, biryani, or kahwa, a substitute will produce a version of the dish that falls short of what it should be. For those preparations, the right answer to the substitute question is simply to have real saffron on hand. For everything else, turmeric or annatto will do the job of adding colour to a dish that does not depend on saffron's specific character.
