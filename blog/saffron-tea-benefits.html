Saffron tea benefits. What the research shows and how to make it

Saffron tea has been prepared across Central Asia, Persia, and South Asia for centuries. What science actually shows about its effects, how to make kahwa and kesar doodh correctly, and why the quality of the saffron determines the entire experience.

Saffron tea has been prepared and consumed across Central Asia, the Middle East, and South Asia for centuries. In Afghan tradition it appears as kahwa, a green tea preparation with saffron, cardamom, and almonds served at gatherings and celebrations. In Persian households it is brewed as a simple golden infusion, drunk in the morning or after meals. In South Asian kitchens it appears as kesar chai and kesar doodh, warm milk preparations given for comfort, recovery, and celebration. The fact that saffron tea appears across so many distinct food cultures is itself informative about what it does.

What the Research Actually Shows

Saffron has been the subject of genuine scientific research, particularly in the past two decades. The compounds responsible for its culinary qualities: crocin, picrocrocin, and safranal, are the same compounds that have been studied in clinical and laboratory settings. The research is promising but requires honest framing. Most studies have used concentrated saffron extracts at doses significantly higher than a cup of tea would provide, and many are preliminary or conducted on small sample sizes. The findings are worth knowing, but they should not be overstated.

The most replicated finding in saffron research concerns mood. Multiple randomised controlled trials have examined saffron supplementation in mild to moderate depression, with several showing results comparable to low-dose antidepressant medication. A 2013 meta-analysis published in Human Psychopharmacology reviewed five such trials and found consistent positive effects. The mechanism is thought to involve serotonin reuptake inhibition by crocin and safranal, similar in principle to how certain antidepressants work. This is not a claim that saffron tea treats depression. Doses used in studies are higher than a typical tea preparation, but the underlying pharmacology is real and documented.

Sleep quality is another area with supporting research. Saffron supplementation has been associated with improved sleep onset and sleep quality in several studies, including a 2020 randomised trial published in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine. Again, the doses studied exceed what a standard cup of saffron tea would contain, but the direction of the finding is consistent with the traditional use of warm saffron milk as a calming evening drink.

Antioxidant properties are well established. Crocin is a powerful carotenoid antioxidant, and saffron extract consistently shows high antioxidant activity in laboratory studies. Whether the concentration in a cup of tea is clinically meaningful is a separate question, but the mechanism is not in dispute.

The traditional use of saffron tea as a warming, calming, and restorative drink is not folklore without basis. The compounds responsible have been studied and the directions of effect are consistent with traditional claims. The gap between traditional use and clinical dosing is real, but smaller than it is for many popular wellness ingredients.

How to Make Saffron Tea

The basic preparation is straightforward. Take six to eight saffron threads and place them in a small mortar with a pinch of sugar. Grind them gently until you have a coarse powder. The sugar helps break down the stigmas and releases the crocin more efficiently than steeping whole threads. Add this to a cup and pour near-boiling water over it, around 85 to 90 degrees Celsius rather than a full rolling boil, which can drive off delicate aroma compounds. Steep for ten to fifteen minutes. The water will turn deep golden and the aroma will develop fully in this time.

Honey is the traditional sweetener and pairs well with saffron's floral character. A small amount of ground cardamom, a quarter teaspoon or less, adds warmth and is the standard addition in Afghan kahwa. Rose water, used sparingly, is common in Persian preparations. None of these additions are required but each has been used alongside saffron across centuries of culinary tradition for good reason.

The Afghan Kahwa Tradition

Kahwa is the traditional Afghan green tea preparation that most prominently features saffron. It is brewed with green tea leaves, whole cardamom pods, a cinnamon stick, and a generous pinch of saffron, then finished with slivered almonds and sometimes rose petals. It is served at weddings, on Eid, at business meetings, and as the standard gesture of hospitality when a guest arrives at a home.

The saffron in kahwa is not incidental. It is central to the character of the drink: its colour, its warmth, and the specific floral note that distinguishes kahwa from any other spiced tea. Afghan saffron from the Herat province, where the climate and soil produce consistently high crocin readings, is considered the appropriate ingredient for a proper kahwa. The difference between a kahwa made with genuine Grade I saffron and one made with low-grade or adulterated product is immediately apparent in both colour and aroma.

Kesar Doodh: Saffron Milk

Kesar doodh is the South Asian preparation of warm milk with saffron, sugar, and often a small amount of ground cardamom or ground almonds. It is given to children before bed, served at celebrations, and used in Ayurvedic tradition as a warming and restorative drink. The saffron is typically bloomed in a small amount of warm milk first, then added to the full cup.

The fat content of whole milk aids in the extraction and absorption of crocin, which is fat-soluble. This is one reason that saffron milk may produce a more pronounced effect than saffron steeped in water alone. The bioavailability of the active compounds is meaningfully different. This is traditional knowledge expressed in practice long before the underlying chemistry was understood.

How Much Saffron to Use

For a single cup of tea: six to ten threads. For kahwa serving four: fifteen to twenty threads. For kesar doodh: eight to twelve threads per cup. These amounts produce a visually golden drink with a clear saffron character. Using fewer threads produces a pale, flavourless result. Using significantly more does not proportionally increase the effect but does affect the flavour, which becomes more intensely bitter at higher concentrations.

Grade I saffron with a crocin reading above 260 will produce a deeper colour and more pronounced flavour at these quantities than lower-grade alternatives. If your saffron tea looks pale yellow after fifteen minutes of steeping, either the quantity is insufficient or the saffron quality is low. Real Grade I saffron at the quantities above produces a drink that is visibly and unmistakably golden.

A Note on Quality

The experience of saffron tea is entirely dependent on the quality of the saffron. Low-grade or adulterated saffron produces a pale, flavourless cup that validates the perception that saffron tea is overhyped. Grade I saffron with verified crocin content produces a drink with genuine character: colour, aroma, and flavour that justify its place in centuries of culinary tradition across multiple cultures.

This is why the sourcing question matters for something as simple as tea. Six threads of genuine Super Negin Afghan saffron in a cup of warm water produces something worth drinking. Six threads of commodity or adulterated product produces coloured water. The ingredient makes the drink.
